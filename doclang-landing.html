The problem

Your documents are lying
to your models.

Modern AI pipelines assume clean, structured input. Real-world documents — contracts, invoices, research papers, regulatory filings — are none of those things. Parsers guess at reading order. Tables become flat text. Figures vanish. Metadata is stripped.

The result: your model's accuracy is bottlenecked by document quality, not model quality. You spend more engineering time wrangling pre-processing than building the product.

What is DocLang

A document representation built for how AI actually reads.

DocLang defines a structured, machine-readable format for documents of any type. Not a converter. Not an API. A standard — like JSON for data, like HTML for the web — that any tool can implement and any pipeline can consume.

Every component in a DocLang asset has explicit type, semantic role, and geometric position. A table is a table. A heading is a heading. A footer is a footer. Your model doesn't have to guess. And compliance metadata — PII flags, AI usage permissions, extraction constraints — travels with the document, not alongside it. The asset knows its own rules.

What DocLang does

  • Preserves reading order and document hierarchy
  • Preserves table structure — rows, columns, merged cells
  • Maps figure and caption relationships
  • Embeds inline metadata and governance annotations
  • Handles multi-column layouts and complex page geometry

Why DocLang

Three things that don't exist anywhere else.

01

Structure you can trust

DocLang maps every component to its semantic role and geometric position. There's no ambiguity about what a piece of content is or where it sits in the document. Your models stop hallucinating structure because the structure is already there.

For business teams: fewer errors in automated document workflows mean fewer manual reviews, lower compliance exposure, and faster time-to-decision.
02

Governance built into the file

Compliance metadata — PII flags, AI usage permissions, extraction constraints — travels with the document, not alongside it. No external policy engine to maintain. No risk of metadata getting stripped in transit.

For business teams: audit-ready by default. Your legal and compliance teams can define rules once, and every downstream system reads them automatically.
03

An open standard anyone can build on

DocLang is governed by the Linux Foundation. The spec is public, the working group is open, and any vendor can implement it. For the first time, the industry has a shared language for documents — one that belongs to no single company and that every tool, pipeline, and model can speak.

For business teams: no lock-in, ever. Swap components as the market evolves. Your documents stay portable because the standard is stable.

FAQ

Honest answers to
obvious questions.

Is this just another document parser?

No. Parsers convert documents into some proprietary output format. DocLang is a standard — a shared specification that any parser, any converter, any AI tool can implement. The goal is interoperability, not another tool to integrate.

What's wrong with just using Docling / FineReader / MyParser instead of DocLang?

Nothing — that's the point. Enable the DocLang output and the structured data they produce stops being tied to your specific pipeline configuration. It becomes consumable by any downstream system that speaks the DocLang standard — your embedder, your LLM, your RAG pipeline, your compliance layer.

Docling and ABBYY FineReader Engine already natively support the DocLang standard.

How is this different from what PDF already contains?

PDF is a presentation format. It tells a renderer where to draw pixels. DocLang is a semantic format — it tells a model what content is. A PDF table and a DocLang table are fundamentally different objects.

Who governs the spec?

The Linux Foundation. The DocLang working group — currently IBM, NVIDIA, Red Hat, and ABBYY — proposes and reviews changes, but the foundation ensures the process remains open and no single vendor controls the roadmap.

Can I contribute?

Yes. The spec, the reference implementation, and the working group processes are all public. Join the GitHub discussion, open an issue, or attend a working group session. The standard improves when more perspectives are in the room.
